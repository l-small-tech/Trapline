# Why Trapline exists

## The short version

Trapline exists for the good of the community. It helps people who depend on the North's
fiber internet turn their everyday experience — the dropouts, the slow evenings, the
mystery outages — into clear, honest, data-supported feedback that our internet provider
can actually act on. A better fiber network benefits everyone, and better feedback is how
we get there.

## Why the fiber network matters so much

In the North, the internet is not a convenience. It is how communities reach doctors,
schools, banks, family, emergency services, and the rest of the country.

Satellite internet has been a genuinely welcome addition — it brings service to places
fiber has not reached and gives many households a real alternative. But satellites are
something we should be able to *choose*, not something we are forced to depend on.
Satellite constellations are owned and operated far from here, their terms and prices can
change overnight, and in a serious conflict they could be degraded, jammed, or destroyed.
If that ever happens, the terrestrial fiber network is what the North falls back on.

That makes fiber critical infrastructure for **northern security, sovereignty, and
resilience**. A fiber network that quietly degrades — because problems go unreported, or
get reported as vague complaints that are easy to dismiss — is a backup that won't be
there when it's needed. Getting the fiber network right, and keeping it right, is worth a
community's sustained attention.

## How Trapline helps

When your internet drops for the fourth time in a week and you call to complain, the
conversation usually stalls at "have you tried rebooting your router?" Anecdotes are easy
to deflect; data is not.

Trapline watches your connection continuously and keeps the receipts. Instead of "the
internet was bad again last night," you can say:

- "The connection dropped 9 times in March, for a total of 3 hours 12 minutes — here is a
  table with the exact time of every incident."
- "During each of those outages my own router stayed reachable while your network did not
  respond — so it wasn't my equipment."
- "Speed tests, run on a schedule (not cherry-picked), averaged 61% of the plan I pay for."

That is feedback an engineer at the ISP can actually work with: specific, time-stamped,
and honest about what was and wasn't the network's fault.

## Fair to the ISP, by design

Trapline is not an ISP-bashing tool. Its credibility — and its usefulness to the network —
depends on being scrupulously fair:

- Problems inside your own home (your router, your WiFi) are detected, labeled as such,
  and **excluded** from claims against the ISP.
- Time when the monitor itself wasn't running is disclosed, never counted as downtime.
- Every report includes the full methodology, and the source code is open — anyone,
  including the ISP, can audit exactly how every number was produced.

The goal is a shared, trustworthy picture of how the network is really performing, so that
problems get found and fixed. When network users and the network operator are looking at
the same honest data, everybody wins — and the North's fiber backbone gets stronger.

## Who this is for

Trapline was built for the Yukon and Northwestel fiber customers, but nothing about it is
Yukon-specific: any community, anywhere, that depends on a terrestrial network and wants
to help improve it can use it as-is.
